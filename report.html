The Quantum Threat to Bitcoin 3
Executive Summary

A Human Rights Imperative

Bitcoin is a financial lifeline for dissidents resisting authoritarian regimes. The rise of cryptographically relevant quantum computers (CRQCs) with the ability to crack Bitcoin's underlying cryptography could threaten the network's security foundations. A team of Google researchers recently published findings representing a quantum computing breakthrough: an algorithm enabled a quantum computer to carry out operations 13,000 times faster than a classical supercomputer.

While quantum computing remains largely theoretical, experts suggest CRQCs could emerge within the next five years. CRQCs could put millions of bitcoin stored in early address formats at risk, and endanger the wider trust that underpins Bitcoin.

The threat has sparked technical, political, and moral debates — including the "burn or steal" dilemma: whether to allow attackers to "steal" funds in early address formats, or "burn" those funds to make them unspendable.

Post-quantum cryptography offers solutions, but migration to quantum-resistant addresses will require years of research, coordination, and consensus in a decentralized and ideologically divided ecosystem.

Key Findings

  • 1.72M bitcoin (~$188B) in early address types, thought dormant or lost, will be highly vulnerable to long-range quantum attacks.
  • 4.49M bitcoin (~$495B) are vulnerable but owners can secure them by migrating to quantum-safe addresses.
  • Preparing Bitcoin for a post-quantum world is a human rights imperative — dissidents can't safely use compromised freedom money.

Drawing on six months of expert discussions, this report explores CRQC risks for dissidents using Bitcoin. Many thanks to HRF Bitcoin Technical Lead, Alex Li, who drove the primary research.

Bitcoin as a Human Rights Tool

Bitcoin as resistance: freedom money that dictators can't stop.

Bitcoin is a powerful tool for safeguarding human rights, promoting financial freedom, and resisting authoritarian control. Tyrannical governments frequently manipulate and surveil their currencies, seeking to silence critics by confiscating property and freezing bank accounts. Dissidents, human rights defenders, journalists, and non-profits under dictatorship face enormous obstacles just to receive donations and pay their bills.

HRF's Financial Freedom program supports Bitcoin as a tool for activists facing financial repression: money that dictators can't stop.

Quantum threats to Bitcoin's classical cryptography are still years away. However, if CRQCs emerge before Bitcoin's cryptographic foundations are upgraded, activists who rely on Bitcoin for secure donations, private savings, and uncensored transactions would find their privacy and safety compromised — and dictators might find ways to steal or stop their money.

Making Bitcoin quantum-proof poses political and social challenges. Its decentralization means any upgrade requires coordination and consensus among a diverse and divided user base. Any change to cryptographic signature schemes requires widespread education for users, developers, node operators, and miners. The community must also consider the 1.72 million bitcoin held in early addresses most vulnerable to CRQCs. Proposed changes have already sparked huge debates over trade-offs between performance, privacy, and backward compatibility.

Technical Landscape

The Current State of Quantum Computing

Quantum computers exist today, but they remain years away from the scale, stability, and precision required to threaten Bitcoin's cryptography. Experts remain divided over whether CRQCs capable of breaking Bitcoin's security will ever emerge. Some emphasize incremental breakthroughs will provide time for solutions; others caution sudden advances could accelerate the timeline.

At this year's Presidio Bitcoin Quantum Summit — convening quantum physicists, Bitcoin Core developers, cryptographers, wallet engineers, miners, and educators — evolving expert views on CRQCs came into sharp focus.

Quantum computing is real. But there are so many things to do. You don't need to worry too much about it, but at the same time, probably it's not a good idea to ignore it… Five to ten years is not a crazy number.
— Sho Suigara, CEO of Blocq
Presidio Bitcoin Quantum Summit — Expert Opinion Shift
Source: Presidio Bitcoin Quantum Summit attendee polling

At the outset, 25% of attendees were unsure whether CRQCs would ever pose a meaningful threat. After two days of intensive discussion, uncertainty dropped to just 8%. The share who believed CRQCs would arrive in 5–20 years jumped from 49% to 69%.

Threat Analysis

Bitcoin's Quantum Threat Vectors

Not all bitcoin will be equally vulnerable. Quantum threats fall into two categories: long-range and short-range attacks, each exploiting different weaknesses in public key exposure.

Long-Range Attacks

A quantum adversary targets bitcoin whose public keys are already exposed — in older address types, re-used addresses, or taproot (P2TR) addresses. Approximately 6.51 million bitcoin (over $718 billion), nearly a third of total supply, is vulnerable.

Active users can protect funds by migrating to quantum-safe addresses — securing 4.49M of the at-risk bitcoin. But the remaining 1.72M bitcoin ($188B), with owners' private keys thought lost, can never be migrated. Satoshi's estimated 1.1M BTC sits in P2PK addresses with exposed public keys.

Short-Range Attacks

These exploit the brief window between a transaction's broadcast and confirmation. A CRQC could intercept an unconfirmed transaction, derive the private key from the exposed public key in real time, and redirect funds. All Bitcoin is vulnerable until post-quantum signatures are adopted.

Bitcoin Vulnerability Breakdown
Someone is going to transition through this phase transition, and it's going to come just as fast as AI came and hit people in the face.
— Terry Rudolph, CEO of PsiQuantum

Modern Bitcoin infrastructure introduces another vulnerability. Most wallets and portfolio trackers store public keys. If these companies are hacked in a CRQC world, attackers could steal users' funds. Hardware wallets, custody providers, and accounting platforms will all need reevaluation.

Solutions

Preparing Bitcoin for the Quantum Era

Quantum-Resistant Signature Schemes

Integrating quantum-resistant signature schemes represents the only durable solution. Two proven approaches exist:

Lattice-Based

More compact; supports multisig, key aggregation, deterministic derivation — features useful for human rights defenders. Introduces new cryptographic assumptions requiring vetting.

10×
larger than current signatures

Examples: CRYSTALS-Dilithium 44, FALCON 512

Hash-Based

Most mature post-quantum option with proven foundations. Larger signatures make key aggregation and multisig more complex.

38×
larger than current signatures

Examples: SPHINCS+, XMSS, Lamport

These dramatic size increases would reduce transactions per block, decrease throughput, and increase storage demands on full nodes. Any effort to increase blocksize or adjust the witness discount is likely to divide the community.

BIP 360

Bitcoin Improvement Proposal 360 is signature scheme-agnostic — it makes taproot addresses more quantum-resistant and provides a flexible framework to accommodate a variety of post-quantum algorithms. Other quantum-resistant BIPs are sure to come.

Implementation

Upgrades to Education & Design

Upgrading Bitcoin is as much a human challenge as a cryptographic one. Any successful soft fork will necessitate user education, thoughtful UI design, and coordination across users, developers, hardware manufacturers, node operators, and civil society.

Wallets are tightly bound to the current elliptic curve model. Quantum-resistant algorithms would introduce larger signatures, slower signing, and complex verification — fundamentally changing how wallets operate. Hardware wallets must adapt while preserving seamless experience.

Many users remain unaware their coins may be vulnerable. Encouraging migration to quantum-safe outputs, when the threat remains abstract, will be difficult. Because Bitcoin has no central authority, every soft fork depends on voluntary adoption and grassroots coordination.

Sufficiently powerful quantum computers are hypothetical, and if they happen, there will likely be a long series of incremental breakthroughs that give us time for more fundamental solutions.
— Pieter Wuille, Bitcoin core contributor

Previous improvements like SegWit took years to reach adoption. Signature schemes that increase transaction sizes 10× or more will trigger debates about block space and scalability.

What Success Requires

A quantum-resilient soft fork must be socially durable as well as technically correct. Consensus is slow, fragile, and precious. Above all, it must remain faithful to Bitcoin's principles: privacy, decentralization, and freedom from coercion.

The Dilemma

Burn, Steal, Hourglass

To protect 1.72 million dormant bitcoin from long-range attack, some in the Bitcoin community advocate a "burn" — rendering quantum-vulnerable coins unspendable after a migration window. Advocates argue this protects monetary integrity, prevents destabilizing wealth redistribution, and reinforces that cryptographic theft is not valid ownership.

Others worry this violates property rights and Bitcoin's promise of immutability. The theft of millions of bitcoin could undermine value for all holders — but so could a unilateral decision to freeze coins.

Conclusion

The Path Forward

The quantum threat to Bitcoin is not imminent — but it is real. The shifting expert consensus at the Presidio summit signals the community must begin preparing now. The stakes are uniquely high for human rights defenders — Bitcoin is often their only tool for receiving funds, preserving savings, and transacting freely.

The solutions exist. BIP 360 provides a flexible framework. But implementation demands social consensus in a decentralized ecosystem where every change is debated and voluntarily adopted.

Above all, a soft fork must remain faithful to Bitcoin's underlying principles: privacy, decentralization, and freedom from coercion.

Anything less risks the financial freedom of the dissidents and human rights defenders who need Bitcoin the most.
